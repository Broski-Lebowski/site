AAReST Cubesat

Optimizing Satellite Antenna Performance

In recent decades, there has been a desire for larger and larger apertures on space telescopes. This makes in-flight assembly a necessity due to the sizing constraints imposed by modern launch vehicles. AAReST, Autonomous Assembly of a Reconfigurable Space Telescope, is a spacecraft based on CubeSat technologies that is to be designed, manufactured, and launched via a collaboration between the California Institute of Technology, the University of Surrey, and the Indian Institute of Space Science and Technology. Although AAReST is a small satellite, measuring only about half a meter on each axis while stowed, it is intended to be a proof of concept for the in-flight assembly of possible future space telescopes with large apertures. It accomplishes this by having three modules that are all able to navigate independently.

For my aerospace engineering minor, I enrolled in Caltech's Ae 105 course in my senior year. In the course's latter half, students are given the opportunity to work directly with the development of AAReST; I chose to work on the telecom team. Prior telecom teams had already come up with the antenna design, as well as selected the radio frequencies AAReST would use. However, the antenna design had not yet been verified experimentally. Thus, my team's goal was to test the antennas, not only making sure that they worked but also that they output a signal strong enough to be picked up by ground stations. To meet this goal, we completed these tasks in order:

  1. Study the sensitivity of antenna performance to spacecraft components
  2. Fabricate an electromagnetic model of the spacecraft
  3. Fabricate the antennas and integrate them with the EM model
  4. Perform the antenna characterization experiment

In RF theory, an ideal antenna is one that is connected to an infinitely large, perfectly conducting ground plane. For satellite antennas, however, the spacecraft itself acts as the ground plane - and it is neither infinitely large nor perfectly conducting. Therefore, experimentation is necessary to characterize the antennas' behavior in this nonideal scenario. Since we could not use actual flight hardware for our experiments, we first needed to fabricate a model of AAReST to use. Because the model only had to be accurate in its electromagnetic field behavior, and not in its physical representation, a faithful replica was not necessary. It was likely that some components of the satellite could be omitted from the model with little to no effect on its EM profile.

To determine which components could be omitted from the model, we carried out several simulations in CST Studio Suite. By first simulating the original, unchanged configuration, and then configurations with different components missing, we could determine if a component was unimportant if its absence did not alter the simulation result significantly. The simulation result we used was the so-called s11 parameter, which describes the proportion of power delivered to the antenna that is reflected back into the spacecraft - the lower the value, the better the signal.

From the simulations, we learned that the camera and boom could be omitted from the model, and the panels could also be much thinner than in actuality, without significantly affecting the accuracy of the model's EM behavior. With this knowledge, I drafted the EM model in SolidWorks, exported the model to 2D DXF files, and used the university machine shop's waterjet cutter to make the panels. Then, I put broke the panels' sharp edges and put them together with masking tape, using expanded polystyrene foam to fill the interior and aluminum flashing to ensure electrical continuity.

Our next task was to fabricate the antennas. They consisted of four components - the monopole antenna, a mechanical bracket, an antenna interface board, and the RF feed wire. The design also called for the antennas to be made out of measuring tape, which has several advantageous properties such as low cost, good electrical conductivity, and tape spring behavior for ease of deployment. After fabricating the antennas with epoxy and solder, they were integrated into the EM model. Since this was the first time the antennas were actually constructed, it took a good amount of trial and error to find a manufacturing process that gave satisfactory results. Nevertheless, once a successful process was found, it was documented for future telecom teams, and we could finally carry out the antenna experiments.

Our antenna tests were carried out using a network analyzer, a device which measures the s11 parameter, also known as the input port voltage reflection coefficient. Recall that the s11 parameter essentially describes the proportion of power delivered to the antenna that is wasted by reflectance back to the radio. Typically, a value of less than -10 dB is considered acceptable. For the first part of our experiment, we had to verify the antennas worked at all. To do this, we calibrated the network analyzer with a known load, then connected the antennas directly to the network analyzer without the EM model present. Once we had verified the antennas were functioning properly, we could carry out the second and main part of the experiment, antenna characterization and tuning. For monopole antennas, the value of the s11 parameter is dependent on the antenna length, with the minimum s11 occurring at higher frequencies the shorter the antenna. Therefore, there should exist an optimum length for maximum power transmission. During antenna fabrication, the measuring tape antennas were cut to much longer lengths than the theoretical optimum lengths. This way, we could find the actual optimum by gradually lessening the length of the antenna, which we did simply by cutting the measuring tape in 0.25" increments, and measuring s11 with the network analyzer after each cut.

Overall, our experiment produced mixed results. On one hand, the experiment for the VHF antenna was an unqualified success. We were able to verify the functionality of the antenna, and also found that the optimum length was very close to what was predicted by theory. On the other hand, no useful results were obtained for the UHF antenna. The reasons for this anomaly were not clear to us - we tried several further experiments to troubleshoot the problem, such as adjusting the interface board contact area and isolating the antenna, but none of them solved the issue. Possible reasons we did not explore are a poor antenna design and EM interference from lab equipment. More work is definitely needed to solve the UHF issue, as well as gather more data such as radiation patterns and s11's in different satellite configurations, which will hopefully be taken up by future telecom teams.
