Magnesium Crystal Twins

An Atomistic Simulation Study of Twin Migration in Magnesium

Introduction

This was a Research Experience for Undergraduates completed at Drexel University, sponsored by the National Research Foundation.

Magnesium, element Mg, has often been called the metal of the future. It is easily recyclable, 33% lighter than aluminum while having similar or better mechanical properties, and is abundant in seawater and the Earth's crust. The adoption of this lightweight metal also has the potential to greatly benefit the environment by increasing the fuel economy of vehicles. These properties make Mg and its alloys prime candidates for structural applications in the automotive and aerospace industries. However, magnesium has a few disadvantages that prevent it from overtaking aluminum as the default lightweight metal, including the fact that it has poor formability and ductility at room temperature, and is prone to fracture and failure.

Magnesium is somewhat unique amongst metals due to its hexagonal close-packed (hcp) crystal structure, which is conducive for the formation of a certain microscopic defect, called twin boundaries. In the crystal structure, twin boundaries are surfaces at which the lattice forms a highly symmetrical structure (often a mirror image) across the boundary surface, resulting in two crystals sharing some of the same lattice points and atoms. It is essential to understand the underlying mechanism of twins to improve the manufacturing process and properties of magnesium alloys, as it is through twin formation that hcp metals deform plastically at room temperature. However, twin behavior is still not well understood. The purpose of this research was to alleviate some of this knowledge deficit.

This study in particular concerned the movement, or migration, of twins through magnesium after formation. When strain is applied to samples of magnesium containing twins, the twin boundary tends to propagate through the sample, one atom at a time. To study this, we used a simulation program called LAMMPS (Large - scale Atomic / Molecular Massively Parallel Simulator), a software developed by Sandia National Laboratories. LAAMPS was run on a computing cluster to model the behavior of twins under various conditions, with each simulation taking about one day to complete. The raw data dumps were then analyzed using several MATLAB scripts for interpretation of the results.

OVITO, Open Visualization Tool, is a commonly used software for atomistic and particulate simulations that lets scientists visualize their simulations, representing each atom as a sphere. The three screenshots above were produced using OVITO, and give an idea of what a twin migration event looks like in the simulation.

The above two images are a representative result from the dozens of simulations that were conducted. It seems that each twin migration event corresponds with a drop in the shear stress, a reasonable occurrence. Also, the time between simulation start and the first twin migration event was longer than the intervals between subsequent migrations. The data for these first events are shown below.

Several trends are visible in the plots. The overall velocity of the twin migration appears to be directly proportional to the strain rate, as expected. Surprisingly, it does not seem to have any correlation with temperature. The time of each first migration event appears to be inversely correlated with the strain rate.

Further research would be conducive to better understanding the exact mechanism of twin movement. I would like to thank NSF REU and Drexel University for this opportunity.
